Lumbar Puncture & CSF Analysis

Clinical reference for ER physicians — indications, technique, troubleshooting, interpretation

Indications for Lumbar Puncture
// When to tap in the emergency setting
Clinical Rule: Any patient with suspected meningitis/encephalitis who is not CT-contraindicated should have LP performed as early as possible. Do not delay antibiotics for LP if CT is required — start empirical treatment first, then tap when safe.
🚨 Urgent / Emergency

Meningitis / Encephalitis

  • Bacterial meningitis (fever + neck stiffness + AMS)
  • Viral encephalitis (HSV, EBV, arbovirus)
  • Fungal meningitis (cryptococcus — immunocompromised)
  • TB meningitis
  • Neurosyphilis
Watch: Meningitis LP
🩸 Hemorrhagic

Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

  • CT negative but thunderclap headache
  • LP at ≥6 hours post-onset to detect xanthochromia
  • "Worst headache of life" / sentinel headache
  • Ottawa SAH Rule positive
Watch: SAH Diagnosis
🧠 Neurological

CNS Conditions

  • IIH — diagnostic & therapeutic drainage
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome (albuminocytologic dissociation)
  • Multiple sclerosis — oligoclonal bands
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus
  • Neurosarcoidosis / leptomeningeal mets
  • NMDA-receptor encephalitis
💉 Therapeutic

Therapeutic Indications

  • Intrathecal chemotherapy / antibiotics
  • Spinal anaesthesia (anaesthesiologist domain)
  • Therapeutic CSF drainage in IIH
  • Epidural steroid injection (adjacent)
Ottawa SAH Rule: LP indicated in headache reaching maximum intensity in <1 hour + any of: age ≥40, neck pain/stiffness, witnessed LOC, onset with exertion, thunderclap, limited neck flexion on exam.
Contraindications
// Know when NOT to tap — safety is non-negotiable
⛔ Absolute

DO NOT Proceed

  • Signs of raised ICP with mass effect: papilloedema, unequal pupils, decerebrate/decorticate posturing
  • CT findings: midline shift, effaced cisterns, posterior fossa mass, obstructive hydrocephalus
  • Skin infection at planned entry site (cellulitis, abscess)
  • Spinal epidural abscess at entry level
  • Coagulopathy: INR >1.4, platelets <50k, active therapeutic anticoagulation
⚠️ Relative — Risk/Benefit

Proceed with Caution

  • Platelets 50–100k: consider transfusion first
  • Anticoagulants: follow bridge protocol
  • Known spinal tumour at entry level
  • Severe lumbar spondylosis (consider fluoroscopy-guided)
  • Prior lumbar surgery at level
  • Agitated / uncooperative patient — consider sedation
CT Before LP Decision
🔬 CT First — Any 1 = Scan First

Do CT Before LP If:

  • New-onset seizure
  • Immunocompromised (HIV, transplant, steroids)
  • Known or suspected CNS lesion
  • Focal neurological deficit
  • Altered consciousness (GCS <15)
  • Papilloedema on fundoscopy
⚠️ No CT available + bacterial meningitis likely → start antibiotics + dexamethasone first, CT when available
✅ May Skip CT If All Normal:

Go Directly to LP

  • Age <60, immunocompetent
  • No papilloedema
  • No focal neurological deficit
  • GCS 15 / normal consciousness
  • No new-onset seizure
✅ All 5 criteria met → LP without CT

Needle Selection
// Type, gauge, and when to use what
Core Principle: Pencil-point (atraumatic) needles significantly reduce post-LP headache (PDPH) by splitting rather than cutting dural fibres. Use them when available. The trade-off is slightly slower CSF flow and requires an introducer needle.

// Needle Tip Comparison — Quincke vs Atraumatic

Standard

Quincke 20G

Classic cutting bevel. Readily available. Faster CSF flow. Higher PDPH rate (~15–30%).

Bevel Orientation (Quincke only): In lateral decubitus, insert with bevel FACING UP (toward ceiling). This orients the cutting edge parallel to the vertically-running dural collagen fibres, splitting rather than transecting them. In sitting position, bevel faces left or right (laterally). This is not relevant for pencil-point needles.

Step-by-Step Technique
// Expand each step
Full Procedure Video

01 Position & Landmark Identification

▼
Lateral decubitus or sitting. Find L3–L4 via Tuffier's line. Mark the interspace with fingernail indent BEFORE sterile draping.

Lateral decubitus tips: Spine must be parallel to bed — no sagittal tilt. Ear–shoulder–hip–knee in one horizontal plane. Pillow between knees helps.

💡 Ask the patient: "Roll toward me and curl up tight." Step behind them, palpate both PSIS simultaneously with both thumbs, draw the mental line, then walk your fingers one space UP to L3–L4.

02 Sterile Prep & Local Anaesthetic

▼
Chlorhexidine in concentric circles — let dry 30 seconds. Apply drapes.

Local anaesthetic sequence:
  1. 25G needle — skin wheal with 1–2 mL 1% lidocaine
  2. 22G needle — infiltrate deeper along intended needle tract, angled toward umbilicus (~15° cephalad), 2–3 mL
  3. Wait 60–90 seconds for full effect
💡 The LA itself is the most painful part for most patients. Inject slowly. If they don't feel the spinal needle going in, you've done it right.

03 Needle Insertion

▼
Midline approach: Bevel UP (Quincke), needle in midline, ~15° cephalad toward umbilicus.

Layers traversed (feel each):
  1. Skin → subcutaneous fat: little resistance
  2. Supraspinous ligament: slight resistance begins
  3. Interspinous ligament: firmer resistance
  4. Ligamentum flavum: dense, rubbery — first "give"
  5. Epidural space: brief space (2–4 mm)
  6. Dura mater: another slight resistance — second "give/pop"
  7. Subarachnoid space: free CSF flow on stylet removal ✓
💡 Remove stylet every 1 cm after passing the ligamentum flavum. Don't overshoot into the anterior venous plexus.
⚠️ Sudden radicular shooting pain → nerve root contact. STOP. Withdraw 5–10 mm, redirect away from affected leg.

04 Opening Pressure

▼
Lateral position only for valid measurement. Attach 3-way stopcock + manometer.

Ask patient to slowly straighten legs slightly (reduces intra-abdominal pressure). Read when CSF column stabilises (5–10 s).

PressureInterpretation
6–20 cmH₂ONormal
>25 cmH₂ORaised ICP — IIH, meningitis, mass
>40 cmH₂OMarkedly raised — drain cautiously, max 20–30 mL
<6 cmH₂OLow — CSF leak, dehydration, spinal block

05 CSF Collection

▼
Let CSF drip freely. Collect 1–2 mL per tube (min 6–8 mL total). Do NOT aspirate with a syringe.

  • Tube 1: Cell count (may be traumatically contaminated)
  • Tube 2: Protein + glucose
  • Tube 3: Gram stain + C&S
  • Tube 4: Special tests + repeat cell count (compare to Tube 1 for traumatic tap)
💡 SAH vs traumatic tap: if Tube 1 and Tube 4 have equal RBC counts = SAH. If RBCs decrease = traumatic tap. Spin Tube 4 — xanthochromic supernatant = SAH.

06 Withdrawal & Aftercare

▼
Replace stylet before withdrawal (reduces PDPH). Withdraw smoothly, apply pressure + dressing.

Post-procedure: No strict bed rest needed — evidence does not support lying flat to prevent headache. Encourage oral fluids. Monitor vitals 1 hour.

Warn about PDPH: Positional headache (worse upright, better supine), onset 24–48 h. Treat: bed rest, hydration, caffeine (300–500 mg), analgesics. Severe: epidural blood patch (gold standard, 85–90% effective). Watch: PDPH & Blood Patch

🦴Needle hits bone / can't advance MOST COMMON▼
Causes: Off-midline, wrong angle, inadequate flexion, calcified midline ligaments (elderly).

Withdraw to just subcutaneous — don't fully remove
2

3
Ensure maximum flexion — push knees further, chin further
4
Try one level higher (L2–L3) or lower (L4–L5)
5
Switch to sitting position — opens gaps significantly
6

Post-LP Complications
// Recognition, timing, and management
Key principle: Most complications are mild and self-limiting. PDPH is the most common. Serious complications (herniation, infection, haematoma) are rare but require prompt recognition.
// COMPLICATION TIMELINE
DURING PROCEDURE
Vasovagal syncope
Bradycardia + hypotension + pallor. Lay patient flat, legs up, IV fluids, atropine if persistent. Prevention: warn patient, calm environment, avoid prolonged painful attempts.
DURING PROCEDURE
Radicular paraesthesia
Transient shooting pain down one leg — nerve root contact. Withdraw and redirect. Document. Persistent neurology post-LP → neurology review.
24–48 HOURS
Post-LP Headache (PDPH)
Most common complication (10–30% with Quincke, 2–5% with atraumatic needles). Classic: bilateral frontal/occipital, positional — worse upright, relieved supine. Manage: bed rest, hydration, caffeine 300–500 mg PO. Persistent (>24h despite conservative) → epidural blood patch (10–20 mL autologous blood, 85–90% success).
24–72 HOURS
Back pain / injection site pain
Common, mild — local tissue trauma. NSAIDs / paracetamol. Severe, progressive, or with radiculopathy → exclude haematoma or abscess (urgent MRI).
DAYS–WEEKS
Spinal / Epidural Haematoma
Rare but serious. Risk factors: coagulopathy, anticoagulants, multiple attempts. Presents: severe back pain + progressive lower limb weakness/bladder dysfunction. Emergency MRI + neurosurgical review. Must decompress within hours to prevent permanent deficit.
DAYS–WEEKS
Meningitis / Abscess
Iatrogenic — failure of sterile technique or skin flora tracking. Presents: fever, worsening headache, neck stiffness post-LP. Extremely rare (<1:1000). Manage as new meningitis.
HOURS (IF CT NOT DONE)
Cerebral Herniation
The feared complication. Occurs if LP performed with unrecognised raised ICP + mass effect. Transtentorial or tonsillar herniation → death. Prevented by CT screening for contraindications. Signs: sudden deterioration, anisocoria, posturing post-LP → immediate CT + neurosurgery.
🩺 PDPH Management Ladder

Post-LP Headache

  • Step 1: Reassurance, oral hydration, paracetamol + NSAIDs
  • Step 2: Caffeine 300 mg PO/IV (vasoconstrictive)
  • Step 3: IV fluids + IV caffeine sodium benzoate
  • Step 4: Epidural blood patch — 10–20 mL autologous blood at LP level. Gold standard for refractory PDPH. 85–90% single-treatment success.
🚨 When to Escalate

Red Flags Post-LP

  • Non-positional headache (may indicate haematoma or herniation)
  • Progressive leg weakness or bladder/bowel dysfunction → urgent MRI
  • Fever + meningism worsening → new meningitis workup
  • Deteriorating GCS post-procedure → immediate CT + neurosurgery
  • Localised back swelling + radiculopathy → exclude haematoma

// SPECIAL TEST GUIDE
🧪 Order by Condition

Additional CSF Tests

  • Cryptococcal Ag: HIV+, immunocompromised
  • HSV/VZV PCR: Encephalitis, temporal lobe
  • Enterovirus PCR: Viral meningitis
  • AFB smear + culture: TB suspicion
  • Oligoclonal bands: MS workup (compare serum)
  • Cytology: Leptomeningeal mets
  • VDRL: Neurosyphilis
  • NMDAR antibodies: Autoimmune encephalitis
  • 14-3-3 / RT-QuIC: CJD
// PITFALLS
⚠️ Traumatic vs SAH

Don't Miss SAH

  • Traumatic: RBCs Tube1 > Tube4, clear supernatant
  • SAH: equal RBCs all tubes, xanthochromic supernatant
  • Xanthochromia needs ≥2h — don't tap too early
⚠️ GBS

Albuminocytologic Dissociation

  • High protein (>0.45, often markedly ↑)
  • Normal or near-normal WBC (<10)
  • May be normal in first 1–2 weeks
  • Repeat LP in 1–2 weeks if GBS strongly suspected
Empirical Treatment — Don't Wait: Bacterial meningitis suspected → Ceftriaxone 2g IV + Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV (reduces hearing loss in H. flu, may reduce mortality in S. pneumo) ± Ampicillin 2g IV if >50 yrs or immunocompromised (Listeria coverage) + Aciclovir 10 mg/kg IV if HSV encephalitis cannot be excluded. Start immediately. LP findings refine, don't delay, treatment.
