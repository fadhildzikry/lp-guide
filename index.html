Lumbar Puncture & CSF Analysis

A clinical reference for ER physicians — indications, technique, troubleshooting, interpretation

Indications for Lumbar Puncture
// When to tap in the emergency setting
Clinical Rule: Any patient with suspected meningitis/encephalitis who is not CT-contraindicated should have LP performed as early as possible. Do not delay antibiotics for LP if CT is required — start empirical treatment first.
🚨 Urgent / Emergency

Meningitis / Encephalitis

  • Bacterial meningitis (fever + neck stiffness + altered mental)
  • Viral encephalitis (HSV, EBV, arbovirus)
  • Fungal meningitis (cryptococcus in immunocompromised)
  • TB meningitis
Watch: Meningitis LP
🩸 Hemorrhagic

Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

  • CT negative but thunderclap headache — LP at 6–12 hrs to detect xanthochromia
  • Classic: "worst headache of life"
  • Gold standard: spectrophotometry for xanthochromia
Watch: SAH Diagnosis
🧠 Neurological

CNS Conditions

  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) — diagnostic & therapeutic
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome (albuminocytologic dissociation)
  • Multiple sclerosis — oligoclonal bands
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus
  • Neurosarcoidosis / neoplastic meningitis
💉 Therapeutic / Other

Therapeutic Indications

  • Intrathecal chemotherapy / antibiotics
  • Spinal anaesthesia (anaesthesiologist role)
  • Therapeutic CSF drainage in IIH
  • Neurosyphilis diagnosis
  • Leptomeningeal metastases
Ottawa SAH Rule: LP is indicated in headache + any of: age ≥40, neck pain/stiffness, witnessed LOC, onset with exertion, thunderclap onset, limited neck flexion.
Contraindications
// Know when NOT to tap — safety first
⛔ Absolute

DO NOT Proceed

  • Signs of raised ICP with mass effect: papilloedema, unequal pupils, posturing
  • CT showing: midline shift, loss of cisterns, posterior fossa mass, obstructive hydrocephalus
  • Skin infection at planned needle site (cellulitis, abscess)
  • Spinal epidural abscess at entry level
  • Coagulopathy: INR >1.4, platelets <50k (relative), active anticoagulation
⚠️ Relative — Assess Risk/Benefit

Proceed with Caution

  • Platelets 50–100k: weigh risk, consider transfusion first
  • Anticoagulants: follow bridge protocols
  • Known spinal cord tumour at planned level
  • Severe lumbar spondylosis/deformity (consider fluoroscopy-guided)
  • Previous spinal surgery at level
  • Uncooperative / agitated patient
When to do CT before LP
🔬 CT First Criteria (any 1 = CT first)

Requires CT Before LP

  • New-onset seizure
  • Immunocompromised
  • Known or suspected CNS lesion (mass, abscess, tumour)
  • Focal neurological deficit
  • Altered consciousness (GCS <15)
  • Papilloedema on fundoscopy
⚠️ If CT unavailable and bacterial meningitis likely → start antibiotics + dexamethasone first, then CT when available
✅ LP Without CT (if all normal)

May Skip CT If:

  • Age <60, immunocompetent
  • No papilloedema
  • No focal neuro deficit
  • Normal consciousness (GCS 15)
  • No new-onset seizure
✅ In this case, skip CT and proceed directly to LP

✓

✓

🪑 Sitting Position (Upright)

Patient sits at edge of bed, hunched forward, hugging a pillow. Excellent landmark identification — ideal for obese patients.

Interspace Selection

Spinal cord ends at L1–L2 in adults. LP is safe below this level. L3–L4 is the standard first choice.

Tuffier's Line (Intercristal Line): Draw an imaginary horizontal line connecting the tops of both posterior iliac crests — this crosses the L4 spinous process (or L4–L5 interspace). Use L3–L4 (one space above) as your primary target.

Step-by-Step Technique
// Expand each step for full detail
Full Procedure Video

01 Position & Landmark Identification

▼
Position: Lateral decubitus — patient on their side, knees to chest, chin tucked to sternum. Back at edge of bed, perpendicular to floor. Ensure the bed is at YOUR comfortable working height.

Find L3-L4: Palpate both posterior superior iliac crests (PSIC). Draw an imaginary line connecting them — Tuffier's line = L4 spinous process. Go ONE space UP to L3-L4, or use L4-L5 as alternative.

💡 Tip: Use your non-dominant thumb to feel the "valley" between spinous processes. Rock your thumb side-to-side to confirm the midline. Mark with a fingernail indent before draping.

02 Sterile Prep & Local Anaesthetic

▼
Sterile field: Wash hands, sterile gloves + gown. Clean skin with chlorhexidine (preferred) or povidone-iodine in concentric circles. Let dry 30 seconds. Apply drapes.

Local anaesthetic:
  1. Use 25G needle, infiltrate skin wheal with 1-2 mL 1% lidocaine at intended insertion point
  2. Switch to 22G needle, inject 2–3 mL deeper along the intended needle tract — direct toward umbilicus, slightly cephalad
  3. Wait 60–90 seconds for full effect
💡 Tip: Tell the patient they'll feel a sting, then pressure, but not sharp pain. If they feel sharp radicular pain later, warn them immediately to tell you.

03 Needle Insertion

▼
Midline approach (standard):
  1. Hold spinal needle bevel UP (parallel to dural fibres = less dural disruption = less headache)
  2. Insert at chosen interspace, in the MIDLINE, angled slightly cephalad (~15° toward umbilicus)
  3. Advance slowly through: skin → supraspinous ligament → interspinous ligament → ligamentum flavum → epidural space → dura mater
  4. Feel the "give" or "pop" as you pass through ligamentum flavum and enter epidural space, then a second subtle pop entering subarachnoid space
  5. In adults: dura is typically 4–7 cm deep (varies with BMI)
💡 Remove stylet every 1 cm after the ligamentum flavum to check for CSF flow — avoids overshooting.
⚠️ If patient reports sudden SHOOTING pain down one leg → you've touched a nerve root. Withdraw 1 cm, redirect slightly away from that leg.

04 Confirming Position & Opening Pressure

▼
CSF flow: Remove stylet — free-flowing clear CSF confirms position. Slow drop-by-drop flow is normal.

Opening pressure (lateral position only):
  1. Attach 3-way stopcock + manometer
  2. Ask patient to slowly straighten legs (without moving spine) — this relaxes abdominal pressure for accurate reading
  3. Read when CSF column stabilises (5–10 seconds)
  4. Normal: 6–20 cmH₂O
  5. >25 cmH₂O = raised ICP (IIH, meningitis, SOL)
  6. <6 cmH₂O = low pressure (may indicate CSF leak)
💡 Opening pressure in sitting position is NOT reliable — convert to lateral if accurate measurement matters (e.g. IIH).

05 CSF Collection

▼
Collect in sequence: Allow CSF to drip into each tube. Collect 1–2 mL per tube (min 6–8 mL total).

  • Tube 1: Cell count (may be contaminated by needle trauma)
  • Tube 2: Protein + glucose
  • Tube 3: Gram stain + culture
  • Tube 4: Special: xanthochromia / PCR / cytology / oligoclonal bands
💡 Send Tubes 1 AND 4 for cell count — if both have equal RBCs, suggests true SAH. If Tube 4 has fewer RBCs, suggests traumatic tap (RBCs "clear" with flow).
⚠️ Don't aspirate with a syringe — this can cause nerve root entrapment or collapse. Let it drip freely.

06 Withdrawal & Aftercare

▼
Withdrawal:
  1. Replace stylet before withdrawing (reduces post-LP headache risk)
  2. Withdraw smoothly in one motion
  3. Apply pressure + adhesive dressing
Post-procedure:
  • No strict bed rest required — evidence does NOT support lying flat to prevent headache
  • Encourage oral hydration
  • Monitor vitals 1 hour post-procedure
  • Observe for: neurological deterioration, bleeding, pain
  • Warn about post-LP headache (positional, 24–48h onset): treat with bed rest, hydration, caffeine, ± epidural blood patch if severe
💡 Post-LP headache: classic = worse upright, relieved lying flat. Distinguished from SAH/meningitis by this postural feature.

Troubleshooting
// Navigating difficult LPs — practical solutions
Watch: Difficult LP Tips
🦴 Needle hits bone / Can't advance MOST COMMON ▼
What happened? Needle hit spinous process (off midline) or lamina (angled wrong).

Causes: Not truly midline, angle too cephalad/caudad, patient not adequately flexed, calcified ligaments (elderly).
1
Withdraw to just under skin (don't fully remove)
2
Re-palpate landmarks. Feel the gap between spinous processes again — you may have drifted
3
Redirect the needle: if too cephalad, aim slightly lower; if off-midline, correct to true midline
4
Ensure patient is MAXIMALLY flexed — ask assistant to push knees further toward chest, tuck chin further
5
Consider switching to sitting position — opens interspinous spaces significantly more
6
Try one space higher (L2-L3) if L3-L4 repeatedly blocked — but stay below L1-L2
7
Consider paramedian (lateral) approach if midline calcified (see below)
Paramedian Approach: Insert 1 cm lateral to midline, angle medially 10–15° and cephalad. Bypasses calcified midline ligaments. Useful in elderly with degenerative spine. Watch: Paramedian Approach
🩸 Bloody CSF / Traumatic tap COMMON ▼
Challenge: Blood in CSF — is it traumatic tap or true SAH?

1
Collect CSF sequentially in Tubes 1–4. In traumatic tap, RBCs decrease tube by tube. In SAH, RBCs are equal across all tubes
2
Spin a sample — traumatic tap: clear supernatant. SAH: xanthochromic (yellow) supernatant
3
Note: xanthochromia takes 2–4 hours to develop after bleed — LP too early (<6h) may miss it
4
If heavily bloody and suspicious: withdraw needle, apply pressure, attempt at a different level (L4-L5) — may get cleaner tap
⚠️ RBC:WBC ratio in traumatic tap ~700:1. More WBCs than expected = true meningeal inflammation on top of traumatic tap.
💧 No CSF flow / Dry tap COMMON ▼
Possible causes: Needle not in subarachnoid space, needle tip against nerve root, needle bevel against dura, dehydration/low pressure, arachnoid adherent to dura.

1
Rotate needle 90° — bevel may be against the dura wall
2
Ask patient to cough or valsalva briefly — may dislodge CSF
3
Advance 1–2 mm further (may not be fully in subarachnoid space)
4
Recheck depth — you may need to go deeper in obese patients (can be 8–10 cm)
5
Replace stylet, withdraw slightly, redirect
6
If still dry: try different interspace or position change, consider ultrasound-guided or fluoroscopy-guided LP
🔍 Ultrasound guidance: Particularly useful in obese patients or those with poor landmarks. Can identify midline, depth to subarachnoid space, and optimal interspace pre-procedure. Watch: Ultrasound-Guided LP
⚡ Electric shock / Radicular pain during procedure CRITICAL STOP ▼
What's happening: Needle tip is contacting a nerve root of the cauda equina. Causes sharp shooting pain down one leg.

1
STOP advancing immediately
2
Ask the patient: which leg? Which direction?
3
Withdraw 5–10 mm and redirect slightly AWAY from the affected side
4
If pain persists: fully withdraw, reassess positioning and midline
5
Document the episode and reassess patient's lower limb neurology post-procedure
⚠️ Persistent paraesthesia post-LP: investigate with neurology. Rare — transient in vast majority of cases.
😰 Obese / Poor landmark patient CHALLENGING ▼
Strategies:

1
Switch to sitting position — landmarks become more palpable when patient is upright and flexed
2
Use ultrasound pre-procedurally to mark midline and measure depth
3
Use a longer spinal needle (standard 3.5"; consider 5" in morbidly obese)
4
Assistant to hold/retract pannus if needed for access
5
If repeated failures: refer to fluoroscopy-guided or CT-guided LP (radiology)
6
For spinal anaesthesia in obese: consider CSE (combined spinal-epidural) approach
🔄 Scoliosis / Spinal deformity CHALLENGING ▼
Considerations: Spinous processes may be rotated, making landmark identification unreliable. Standard midline approach may be misleading.

1
Palpate carefully — identify the true interlaminar space, not just the rotated spinous process
2
Use ultrasound to identify true midline and target interspace
3
Approach from the concave side of the scoliosis (wider interspinous space)
4
Consider fluoroscopy-guided if severe or repeated failures

// COMMON PITFALLS
⚠️ Don't Miss

Traumatic Tap vs SAH

  • Traumatic: RBCs decrease tube 1→4, clear supernatant on spin
  • SAH: Equal RBCs all tubes, xanthochromic supernatant
  • Xanthochromia requires ≥2 hours post-bleed to develop
  • D-dimer in CSF may help but not widely standardised
🚨 Urgent Action

Bacterial Meningitis Pattern

  • Neutrophilic pleocytosis + low glucose + high protein
  • Don't wait for culture — start empiric treatment
  • Ceftriaxone + Dexamethasone + ± Ampicillin (age >50 or immunocomp)
  • + Aciclovir if HSV cannot be excluded
// SPECIAL TESTS BY CONDITION
🧪 Order When Suspected

Additional CSF Tests

  • Cryptococcal Ag: Immunocompromised, HIV+
  • HSV/VZV PCR: Encephalitis, temporal lobe involvement
  • Enterovirus PCR: Viral meningitis
  • AFB smear + culture: TB suspicion
  • Oligoclonal bands: MS workup
  • Cytology: Leptomeningeal mets, haematological malignancy
  • VDRL: Neurosyphilis
  • 14-3-3 protein / RT-QuIC: CJD
GBS — Albuminocytologic Dissociation: High protein (>0.45 g/L, often markedly elevated) with normal or near-normal cell count (<10 cells/µL). Classic for Guillain-Barré syndrome. Remember: may be normal in first 1–2 weeks of illness.
